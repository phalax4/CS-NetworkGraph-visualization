Page Rank & Zipf's Law

The Rich get Richer - Herbert Simon

Zipf's law is the statistical occurrence where a few entities gather the most of a distribution while the majority of entities have very little of it. In information retrieval this translates to the long-tailed curve on the left, where there are a few words that occur many times and the majority of words are very rare in occurence. Herbet Simon aptly connects Zipf's law to the real world. Those with great wealth, become even more wealthy at a faster rate, while the majority of people will likely never be ultra wealthy. Not only does Zipf's law apply to statitical analysis of text corpuses, but it also helps explain popularity, social networks, populations and many other organically growing phenonmons.

Man is by nature a social animal - Aristotle

How does Zipf's Law apply to Page Rank then?

Page Rank can be thought of as a way to measure popularity. More popular pages get rated up more and more, getting most of the page rank score while the majority of nodes only get the minimum score. Popularity in relation to page rank can be thought of as many authoritative pages pointing to a another page (making it popular). Clearly a web graph will exhibit Zipf's Law by clustering into different spheres. This is even more true for social networks, where social creatures will form groups. We can see it in our society, with viral videos/posts and popular youtube stars. Popularity begets more popularity. When Zipf's law applies to graphs, it can be said the graph is a Free-Scale graph. This was first investigated by Stanely Milgram who fathered the concept of 6-degrees of separation. 6 degrees of separation of nodes which can be thought of as people is a property of Scale-Free graphs. Page rank then can effectively measure which nodes will gather the most clusterings around it. The graph above shows dense clustering interspersed by the rare occasional links connecting dense clustering together, demonstrating this.
